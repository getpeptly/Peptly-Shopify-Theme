# peptly — brand style guide

**Version 1.0 | April 2026**

This document defines how peptly looks, sounds, and feels across every customer touchpoint. Anyone creating content for the brand — you, future contractors, agencies, designers — should start here. When in doubt, default to the rules in this document.

---

## 1. Brand essence

**Positioning in one sentence:** peptly is the first physician-supervised peptide therapy brand priced for the people who actually want it.

**Three words that define the brand:** legitimate, accessible, confident.

**The feeling we want customers to have when they see our brand:** "Finally — someone took this seriously without making it intimidating or unaffordable."

## 2. The wordmark

### Primary wordmark

**peptly** — set in all lowercase, single word, no period.

This is the only acceptable form of the brand name in all branded contexts. The all-lowercase treatment signals modern, approachable, and digital-native — aligning peptly with brands like hims, ro, oura, and olipop, and visually distancing it from clinical pharma brands and overly corporate health companies.

### Wordmark rules

- **Always lowercase.** Never PEPTLY, never Peptly, never PEPTly.
- **Never abbreviated.** Not "PPT," not "P," not "ptly."
- **Never combined with other words inside the wordmark.** Not "peptly health," not "peptly rx," not "peptly md." If you need a descriptor, place it below or beside the wordmark, never inside it.
- **Always one word.** Never "pept ly" or "pept-ly."
- **No bold, italic, or underline within the wordmark.** The wordmark is a fixed asset, not a piece of body text.

### Clear space

The minimum clear space around the wordmark is equal to the height of the letter "p" in "peptly." Nothing — no other text, logos, images, or graphic elements — should encroach on this space.

### Minimum size

- Digital: 80 pixels wide minimum
- Print: 0.75 inches wide minimum

Below these sizes the wordmark loses legibility and should not be used. For very small applications (favicons, app icons, social media profile pictures), use the icon mark instead.

### Icon mark

The icon mark is a simplified version of the brand for use where the full wordmark won't fit or won't read clearly: a single lowercase "p" set in Inter Medium, in bone (#F5F1EA) on a deep forest (#1F3A2E) rounded square background. Use cases include favicons, app icons, social media profile pictures, and any small-scale application where the full wordmark won't fit.

## 3. Color palette

The palette is intentionally restrained. Three core colors plus two accents. This restraint is itself a brand signal — clinical brands use too many colors, wellness brands use too few. peptly sits in the middle.

### Primary colors

**Deep forest** — `#1F3A2E`
The dominant brand color. Used for the wordmark, primary backgrounds, headlines, and trust-signaling elements. Forest green communicates biology, calm authority, and natural health without being literal about it. Avoids the cliché "medical blue."

**Bone** — `#F5F1EA`
The primary background color. A warm off-white that reads premium without being sterile. Use this instead of pure white for website backgrounds, product packaging, and any large surface area. Pure white feels clinical; bone feels considered.

**Ink** — `#1A1A1A`
The primary text color. A near-black that feels softer than pure #000000 but still reads as confident and authoritative. Use for body copy, headlines on light backgrounds, and any high-contrast element.

### Accent colors

**Copper** — `#B8743E`
A warm, premium accent used sparingly for highlights, calls-to-action, and moments where the brand needs to feel slightly elevated. Never as a dominant color. Think: button hover states, small underlines, subtle highlights in editorial layouts.

**Mist** — `#D4D9D2`
A muted sage gray for secondary text, dividers, borders, and supporting elements. The "quiet" color that makes the rest of the palette work.

### Color usage rules

- **70/20/10 rule.** Roughly 70% of any layout should use bone (background), 20% should use ink and deep forest (text and structure), 10% should use copper and mist (accents and supporting elements). This proportion creates the calm, premium feeling the brand needs.
- **Never use red, bright orange, or neon green.** These colors are owned by pharma warning labels, fast food, and supplement brands. They are off-brand for peptly.
- **Never use pure white (#FFFFFF) for backgrounds.** Always use bone. Pure white reads clinical and breaks the brand feel.
- **Never use pure black (#000000) for text.** Always use ink. Pure black is harsh against bone.
- **No gradients.** Flat colors only. Gradients feel dated and supplement-brand.
- **Color is for restraint, not decoration.** When in doubt, use less color, not more.

## 4. Typography

### Primary typeface

**Inter** — Google Font, free.

Used for everything — headlines, body copy, navigation, buttons, captions. Inter is highly readable at small sizes, has excellent screen rendering, and pairs with itself across the entire system. Using a single typeface family keeps the brand cohesive and reduces operational overhead.

### Type scale

| Element | Size | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Display (hero headlines) | 48–64px | 500 (medium) |
| H1 | 36–42px | 500 |
| H2 | 28–32px | 500 |
| H3 | 22–24px | 500 |
| Body large | 18px | 400 (regular) |
| Body | 16px | 400 |
| Caption | 14px | 400 |
| Micro (legal, footnotes) | 12px | 400 |

### Typography rules

- **Two weights only.** Regular (400) and Medium (500). Never Bold (700), never Light (300). Two weights is enough for hierarchy without feeling heavy.
- **Sentence case only.** Never Title Case, never ALL CAPS. The brand voice is calm and considered, not shouting. Apply this everywhere — headlines, buttons, navigation, social posts.
- **Generous line height.** Body copy uses 1.6–1.7 line height. Headlines use 1.2–1.3. Tight line height feels cramped; generous line height feels premium.
- **Maximum line length: 70 characters.** Lines longer than this become hard to read and feel like a wall of text.
- **No italic in body copy.** Reserve italic for emphasis on rare occasions, never as a styling default.

## 5. Imagery direction

The brand's visual world is the single fastest way customers form an opinion. Get this wrong and the positioning falls apart no matter how good the copy is.

### What's on-brand

**Real people, natural lighting, quiet moments.** A man in his 40s tying his running shoes at sunrise. A woman doing physical therapy. Hands holding a vial. Close-ups of texture, skin, recovery. The visual world of peptly is calm, observed, real.

**Clinical objects shot beautifully.** A compounding pharmacy vial photographed like a luxury good. A prescription label shot in soft light. The science made aspirational.

**Nature and biology references.** Plants, water, organic textures. Visual metaphors for healing and recovery without being literal.

**Editorial photography.** Think New York Times Magazine, Cereal, Kinfolk. Considered, slightly desaturated, never overproduced.

### What's off-brand

- **Stock photos of doctors in white coats.** Cliché, distrusted, signals fake legitimacy.
- **Before/after weight loss images.** Owned by Medvi and the entire shady GLP-1 space. Avoid completely.
- **Smiling models on white backgrounds.** Generic supplement aesthetic.
- **Gym selfies and flexing.** Wrong audience signal — peptly is for serious recovery, not gym bros.
- **AI-generated people.** Both off-brand and a regulatory risk. The whole Medvi controversy started here. Use real photography only.
- **Stock medical imagery.** Test tubes, double helixes, microscopes, pills falling in slow motion. All off-limits.
- **Bright, saturated, high-energy imagery.** The brand is calm. Imagery should be calm.

### Photography rules

- **Always real photography or commissioned illustration.** Never AI-generated images of people or fake testimonials.
- **Natural light preferred over studio light.** When studio is needed, soft and directional only.
- **Slightly desaturated edit.** Push shadows up, pull highlights down, mute saturation by 10–15%.
- **Faces are okay but never the focus.** The product, the moment, the texture is the focus. Faces support, they don't lead.

## 6. Voice and tone

### Voice (constant)

peptly speaks like a knowledgeable friend who happens to be a scientist. Confident in the facts, generous with explanations, never condescending, never hyped.

**Always:**
- Direct and clear
- Informed and specific
- Calm and measured
- Honest about limitations
- Respectful of the customer's intelligence

**Never:**
- Hyped or salesy
- Vague or hand-wavy
- Defensive or apologetic
- Condescending or oversimplified
- Dramatic or urgent

### Tone (varies by context)

The voice stays the same, but the tone shifts based on where the customer is:

- **Educational content (blog, social, FAQ):** Patient, thorough, generous. Take the time to explain.
- **Marketing copy (ads, landing pages):** Confident, clear, benefit-led. Get to the point.
- **Customer support:** Warm, calm, helpful. Solve the problem without making the customer feel bad.
- **Legal and medical disclaimers:** Plainspoken, not corporate. Use real language, not legalese.

### Sentence-level rules

- **Use short sentences.** Short sentences feel confident. Long sentences feel like hedging.
- **Use specific numbers.** "Most customers see results in 4–6 weeks" is better than "results may vary."
- **Cut adverbs.** "Very effective" is weaker than "effective." "Truly transparent" is weaker than "transparent."
- **No exclamation points.** Ever. Excitement is for amateurs.
- **No emojis in branded content.** Customers can use them. The brand doesn't.
- **Avoid hedge words.** Strike "may," "might," "could potentially," "is believed to" wherever possible. Either say it or don't.

### Words and phrases we use

- Prescribed
- Physician-supervised
- Compounded
- Protocol
- Recovery
- Optimized
- Transparent
- Legitimate

### Words and phrases we don't use

- Miracle, breakthrough, revolutionary, game-changer
- Natural (overused, meaningless)
- Wellness journey, transformation, life-changing
- Cutting-edge, next-generation, advanced
- Boost, supercharge, unlock, unleash
- Doctor-approved (vague, regulator-flagged)
- Clinically proven (regulator-flagged unless we have the studies)
- 100% safe, completely safe, no side effects (medically false and regulator-flagged)

## 8. The five-second test

Before publishing anything — an ad, a post, a page, an email — read it and ask:

1. **Does it sound like a friend who happens to be a scientist?** If it sounds like a salesperson or a pharmaceutical company, rewrite it.
2. **Would a smart 40-year-old trust this?** If it feels hyped, dumbed-down, or fake, rewrite it.
3. **Is it specific?** If it uses vague claims, replace them with specifics or remove them.
4. **Does it match the visual rules?** If colors, fonts, or imagery violate the system, fix them.
5. **Would Medvi publish this?** If yes, rewrite it. We are deliberately not Medvi.

If it passes all five, ship it. If it fails any, fix it before publishing.
